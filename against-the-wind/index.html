Against the Wind

A sailboat can sail toward the wind. That is not obvious, and for most of naval history it was barely true. Drag the wind around, steer, and watch the forces that make it work — then switch to a Napoleonic square-rigger and watch them stop working.

Why the square rig can't

Switch the rig and watch the grey wedge yawn open. A square sail's chord lies along its yard, and the yard fouls the shrouds long before it comes round: "in most ships the sails make with the keel an angle of 40 degrees, or thereabouts, when close-hauled" (Steel, 1795). A sloop sheets to 7°. That one difference, plus the drag of all that rigging, is the whole story — and it is why the ship holding the weather gauge, the upwind position, decided whether a battle happened at all.

What you're looking at

The sail is a wing. Air flowing across it is turned, and the sail is pushed perpendicular to the airflow — that push is lift, and it is much larger than the drag pulling the boat downwind. Lift points mostly sideways, which by itself would just shove the boat across the water. It doesn't, because the keel is also a wing — a wing in water, which is 800 times denser than air. A slip of only a few degrees (the leeway you can watch in the readout) is enough for the keel to cancel almost all of that sideways push.

What survives the cancellation is a small forward component: net thrust. Turn on the vectors and you can watch it happen. The boat is squeezed forward between two wings pushing in nearly opposite directions — like a wet watermelon seed pinched between finger and thumb.

Sail closer and closer to the wind and the angle of attack on the sail shrinks until the lift can no longer pay for the drag. Then thrust goes to zero, the boat stops, and the sails shiver: in irons. That dead wedge is why nobody sails straight at the wind — you zig-zag around it, and the polar diagram tells you the best angle to zig at.

Things worth trying

  • Turn off auto-trim and over-sheet the sail. Watch the angle of attack climb past stall — lift collapses, drag explodes, and the boat slows down while the sail is pulled in harder. Every new sailor does this.
  • Watch the apparent wind. As the boat accelerates it drags the wind forward — the blue arrow swings toward the bow. On a fast reach you end up sailing into a wind that partly doesn't exist until you make it.
  • Trace the full polar on each rig and compare. The sloop's fastest point of sail is a reach, across the wind. The square rig's is downwind — because past the stall its sail isn't a wing at all, just a very large, very effective bag.

Is this honest?

That is the only question that matters for a thing like this, so here is the full answer. Nothing on this page is drawn because it ought to look that way. The polar curve, the no-go wedge, the stall, the in-irons state — all of it is computed, every frame, from forces. If the physics were wrong, the picture would be wrong, and you would be able to catch me.

The numbers were checked by a fleet of research agents working to one standing instruction: write UNVERIFIED rather than guess. They used it, repeatedly and inconveniently, and what came back is set out claim by claim in the fact sheet. Three kinds of number went into the model, and they do not have the same standing:

  • Measured — the square rig's sail curve is real wind-tunnel data: a six-sail square foremast, tested as a model of the training ship Nippon Maru, peaks at a lift coefficient of about 1.0 and does not stall until 30° (Amemiya et al., 1992). The bracing limit is Steel's 40°. Air density, seawater density, the ITTC friction line, induced drag as CL²/(π·AR·e), Froude scaling — all sourced.
  • Fitted — the rig's windage. No source anyone could reach apportions the drag of a square-rigger's masts, yards and standing rigging. It is therefore the model's one free parameter, tuned so that the ship reproduces the six-point wall. Which makes it a prediction, and a checkable one: for the arithmetic to work, that rigging has to drag like roughly 250 m² of flat plate.
  • Unsourced, and flagged as such — the sloop's sail curve, and any published polar to calibrate her against. ORC's certificate database, which would have both, is behind a login. So the sloop's speeds are the model's prediction, not a fit to data. She comes out beating at 49° and topping out at 5.9 knots against a 6.0-knot hull speed — which is roughly where a boat of that size really lives. Treat that as a check she passed, not a target she was aimed at.

One result deserves its own paragraph, because it is the reason this page exists. The square rig's six-point limit is set nowhere in the code. The model is told only that her sails brace no closer than 40° to the keel — Steel, writing in 1795, with no idea what he was calibrating. Run the forces and the wall appears on its own at 69°. Six points is 67.5°. Count her leeway and she makes good 73.4°; Blunt, in 1824, worked the same figure out to 72.5–73.5°. Nobody told the simulation any of that. It is two-hundred-year-old seamanship and a page of aerodynamics arriving at the same number from opposite directions, and it is the strongest evidence I can offer you that the thing is not lying.

And because the sloop's sail curve is the one thing here that was fitted rather than measured, the self-test ends by taking it away from her: hand the sloop the square rig's own measured, inferior wind-tunnel curve and she still beats at 52° against the square rig's 69°. The modern boat's advantage isn't a number I chose. It is that she can sheet her sail to 7° off the centreline, and the frigate cannot get within 40°.

What this model leaves out

Being honest includes being honest about the simplifications, so: no heel (a real boat lays over, which spills wind and changes everything), no waves, current or tide, flat water, quasi-static forces (no momentum in the sail, no dynamic stall), one aggregate sail per rig rather than a real multi-sail trim problem, and no rudder drag. The square-rigger is modelled as a generic Napoleonic frigate, not any specific ship. This is a model that gets the reasons right; it is not a velocity-prediction program you should race with.
